Trust & security: what PayeeProof does, what it does not do.

PayeeProof is designed to reduce wrong-address, wrong-network, and ambiguous-destination mistakes before funds move. The safest version of this product is simple: it does not hold funds, it does not execute transfers, and it gives operators a clear decision record instead of hand-wavy reassurance.

Core principle

PayeeProof should be a control layer, not a new trust problem.

What changes

Teams get a structured pre-send decision, a reason code, a destination classification, and a clear next step before approval.

What does not change

The payout owner still owns final approval, operational policy, risk tolerance, and escalation rules.

Operating model

Designed to verify before approval, not to sit in the middle of funds.

No custody

PayeeProof does not store, hold, or control customer funds. It is a verification layer, not a wallet, exchange, or payment processor.

No fund movement

PayeeProof does not send transactions on behalf of the user. The operator or payout platform remains the execution point.

Clear operator decisions

The product is built to output practical decisions such as SAFE, BLOCK, REVERIFY, or TEST FIRST — with a reason and operator-facing next step.

Live checks, not static guesses

Recipient type and transaction context are derived from live chain lookups and rule-based review, not only from a form field looking syntactically valid.

Scoped use case

The initial focus is transfer safety around addresses, networks, destinations, and post-error guidance — not broad AML, sanctions, or fraud scoring.

Explainable outputs

Every serious control layer should explain why it returned an answer. That is why decision records include verdicts, destination type, confidence, and reason codes.

Boundaries

What PayeeProof checks — and what the payout team still owns.

✓ What PayeeProof is meant to check

  • Address validity for the selected network
  • Whether the destination looks like a personal wallet, contract, exchange-like deposit, router, or unknown destination
  • Whether the selected asset and network combination should be blocked, reverified, or tested first
  • Whether a submitted transaction can be found on-chain and summarized for recovery guidance
  • Whether the verification result can be expressed in a structured record for ops, support, or audit review

→ What the payout team still owns

  • Final approval of the transfer
  • Internal payout policy and exception handling
  • Escalation decisions for ambiguous or unsupported cases
  • Sanctions, KYC, fraud, or broader compliance controls outside PayeeProof’s current scope
  • Customer communication, account ownership checks, and platform-side entitlement rules
Pilot handling

Pilot reviews should feel controlled, limited, and easy to evaluate.

1. Narrow use case first

Pilots start with a specific transfer or support workflow: pre-send approval, destination review, or transaction recovery guidance. This keeps evaluation concrete.

2. Structured outputs

The team reviews what a real record looks like: checked_at, verdict, reason_code, confidence, destination class, and recommended next action.

3. Human-readable review

Pages such as Policy examples, Pilot flow, and the public sample record exist so non-engineering stakeholders can understand the decision layer quickly.

Practical pilot posture

A strong pilot does not ask a partner to trust vague claims. It gives them a narrow workflow, a sample record, clear verdict semantics, and a contact path for follow-up questions.

FAQ

Short answers to the trust questions a serious buyer will ask.

Does PayeeProof move funds?

No. It is designed as a verification and guidance layer. Execution remains with the payout owner or platform.

Does PayeeProof replace payout policy?

No. It supports policy with structured decisions, but the team still controls approval logic, exceptions, and escalation.

Is this a generic risk engine?

No. The current scope is transfer safety: address, network, destination, and transaction-guidance decisions around crypto transfers.

Why keep this page separate from the main site?

Because buyers need trust material without turning the homepage into a dense technical console. This page is the trust layer; the homepage stays commercial.

What should a pilot reviewer look at first?

The public sample verification record, the policy examples page, and the pilot flow page. Those three together explain the decision layer clearly.

How do we start?

Send the payout or support workflow you want to protect, the networks involved, and the type of destination ambiguity you want to reduce.
